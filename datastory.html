Exploring Happiness: Data Insights into Key Influencing Factors

Visualization 1: This chart displays the rankings of the top five happiest and least happy countries globally in 2021.

Visualization 2: The world map shows the happiness index of various countries from 2005 to 2020, with a sliding interactive slider that presents a geographic gradient heat map for each year. Over time, it is evident that some countries, such as Russia and China, have significantly improved their happiness index. In contrast, some African countries have shown slow changes in their happiness index.

Visualization 3: The correlation heat map illustrates the relationship between the happiness index and other factors, revealing a strong positive correlation between the Ladder Score and variables such as Logged GDP per capita (per capita GDP), Social support, and Healthy life expectancy. Freedom to make life choices also shows a certain positive correlation with the Ladder Score. Conversely, Generosity, Youth 15-24 Literacy Rate Female/Male, and Unemployment have a weaker correlation with the Ladder Score.

Visualization 4: According to these visualization results, the following three images further compare several factors that have a higher correlation with the happiness index. Based on regional division and population as a limiting factor, it is observed that countries and regions with lower per capita GDP, health, and social support generally have a lower happiness index. Western European countries consistently maintain a higher happiness index. Population size is also a consideration; India and China have large populations, which impact their overall happiness index, maintaining a moderate level. Does a lower population density lead to an increase in happiness? Countries with smaller populations, such as Finland and Sweden, face less pressure on natural resources, thereby helping to maintain environmental quality, and resources and public services may be more manageable and distributed. Additionally, a larger population size is usually associated with a larger economic scale and more job opportunities, which could positively affect happiness. Thus, population is a factor worth considering.
